Measurement

Lesson One · 4 min

What Gets
measured

What gets measured, gets managed.

Old business saying, and the entire reason this pillar exists. You cannot manage what you have never actually looked at.

How I found it

I Spent Years Trying to Outrun my fork

01 / 04

I Was Training Hard

Muay Thai sessions burning a thousand calories. Running. Cardio stacked on cardio. I worked as hard as anybody I knew, and for a long time it was enough to cover for everything else.

02 / 04

My Diet Was Never Handled

I was not paying attention to what was going into my body or in what portions. I did not have to. The volume of work I was doing hid the problem. So I never learned the skill.

03 / 04

Then Life Changed and the Cover Was Gone

The training volume of your twenties and thirties does not survive your forties. Work, family, recovery, time. When the output dropped, the diet I had been outrunning caught up to me all at once.

That was the wall. Not a broken metabolism. A lifestyle that changed while my habits did not.

04 / 04

One Skill Fixed It

Three or four years ago I learned to weigh my food and know my numbers. That is it. That is the whole thing. I am the leanest, strongest, best conditioned I have ever been, and I am 49.

Training was never my problem. Measurement was.

Lesson Two · 7 min

The First
number

Before you change your diet. Before you buy a supplement. Before you cut a single carb. There is one number everything else depends on, and almost nobody finds it.

The pattern

Everyone Starts at Step three

  • ✕New diet on Monday
  • ✕Supplements in the cart
  • ✕Carbs cut to zero
  • ✕More cardio than ever
All of it depends on a number most people never find.
The number

Your Maintenance calories

TDEE. Total Daily Energy Expenditure. The calories your body burns across a full day of training, working, walking, and living. Your baseline.

Eat above it, you gain. Eat below it, you lose. Eat at it, you hold. Never find it, and you are guessing at everything downstream forever.

It is an estimate, not a verdict. No formula knows your exact body. What it gives you is a starting point far closer than a guess, and something concrete to adjust from once you have real data.

The math

How the Number Is built

BMR is what you would burn lying in bed all day. Age, height, weight, and sex go in, a number comes out. The formula worth using is Mifflin-St Jeor.

Activity is the multiplier, and it is where almost everyone gets it wrong. Someone getting four to six thousand steps a day needs far less than someone hitting the gym hard and walking ten to fifteen thousand.

The direction

You pick one. Chasing two at once usually means progress on neither.

Ten percent is not dramatic, and that is the point. Aggressive cuts after 40 cost you energy, cost you training quality, and cost you muscle. You cannot afford to give muscle back at this age. Start at ten and earn your way down only if progress genuinely stalls.

The anchor

Protein Comes first

1g
Per pound of bodyweight

Non negotiable after 40. Muscle is the asset that protects your metabolism, your strength, your joints, and your independence later. Protein is what protects the muscle.

Set it first. Build every meal around the protein source, then fill in the rest. If you hit nothing else in a day, hit this.

One more thing

Your Number Is yours

Do not compare it to anybody else's. Not your training partner's, not your spouse's, not somebody's in the community.

Two people the same age and the same height can have targets hundreds of calories apart, and both are correct. Bodyweight, activity, and goal all move the number. A higher target does not mean somebody is doing better and a lower one does not mean you are behind.

The only number that matters to you is the one on your screen, and the only comparison worth making is you now against you three weeks from now.

Lesson Three · 6 min

Carbs Are
the lever

Three macros. Two are locked. One is the only dial you ever need to turn.

Protein
🔒 Locked at 1g / lb
Fat
🔒 Locked at 30%
Carbs
The Lever⬆⬇

Protein stays put because muscle depends on it, and after 40 muscle is the asset you cannot afford to lose. It does not drop when calories drop. In a deficit it matters more, not less.

Fat stays put at 30 percent because hormones, joints, and staying full depend on it. Cutting fat to nothing is how people end up miserable, stiff, and hungry.

That leaves carbs. Whatever calories are left after protein and fat get covered, carbs take. Which means carbs are the only thing that ever needs to move.

⬆ Carbs Up
Heavy training day. Energy in the tank is low. Lifts are dragging. Add 25 to 50g of carbs and fuel the work. Carbs are not the enemy, they are the fuel that lets you train hard enough to keep the muscle.
⬇ Carbs Down
Three to four weeks of honest tracking and the scale has not moved. Pull 25 to 50g of carbs. Not protein. Not fat. Carbs. Then give it another three weeks before you touch it again.
One dial. Up for fuel. Down for fat loss. that's the system.

Why This Beats a New Diet

Every eight weeks somebody sells you a new plan. Keto, carnivore, fasting, cleanses. All of it is just a different way of eating fewer calories with extra rules attached.

You do not need a new plan. You need one lever and the discipline to use it. When something stalls, you know exactly what to move and by how much. That is a system you can run for the rest of your life instead of a diet you quit in March.

Small Moves Only

The calculator has a fine-tune control built into it. Plus or minus 100 calories at a time, capped at 300 total. That cap is deliberate.

If you think you need to move more than 300 calories from where the formula put you, the problem is almost never the formula. It is the tracking. Go back and weigh honestly for one week before you touch the number again.

The Fourth
number

Calories, protein, carbs, fat. Then everybody stops counting. There is a fourth number, and almost nobody tracks it.

It Lives Inside Your Carbs

This is the part that confuses people. Fiber is not additional grams stacked on top of your carbs. It is already counted inside them.

So hitting your fiber target does not mean eating more. It means making your carbs work double duty. Choosing sources that carry fiber with them instead of sources that do not.

Why It Matters More After 40

Digestion slows. Cholesterol becomes something your doctor actually mentions. Blood sugar control gets less forgiving. Fiber helps all three.

And there is a practical one nobody talks about: fiber keeps you full. When you are eating in a deficit, that is not a small thing. It is often the difference between sticking to the plan and raiding the pantry at nine at night.

How to Hit It

Vegetables. Fruit. Beans and lentils. Whole grains over refined. Berries are the highest-fiber fruit by a wide margin.

You do not need a supplement to get there, though I use one in the morning because it is easy. Food first, supplement to close the gap.

Lesson Six · 8 min

The Chipotle
method

The skill everybody thinks is tedious. It takes two to five minutes and it is the one that changes everything.

"Ugh, do I really have to weigh my food?"

That is the single most common thing I hear when tracking comes up. I understand it. It sounds like homework, it sounds tedious, and it sounds like something that will take over your life.

It is none of those things. But you will not believe me until you weigh out one real serving of peanut butter and see what a tablespoon actually looks like. Do that once and the argument is over.

The method

Build It Like a chipotle bowl

This is the whole trick, and it is why weighing food is easy for me and hard for most people.

Do not try to weigh a finished casserole. Do not try to reverse engineer a mixed dish. Build your meal one ingredient at a time, into one bowl, on top of the scale. Weigh, log, zero, next ingredient. Exactly like watching someone build a bowl at Chipotle.

Each ingredient gets logged the second it goes in, so you never have to remember a single number.

1
Bowl on the scale, then zero it
Always tare before the first ingredient so you are weighing food and not dishware.
2
Add one ingredient
Just one. Use grams, not ounces. Grams are more precise and every food label supports them.
3
Log it before you add anything else
Type the number into the app while it is on the screen in front of you. Do not batch this.
4
Zero the scale again
Now the next ingredient weighs clean on top of what is already in the bowl.
5
Repeat until the meal is built
Save your regulars as favorites in the app. After a week this takes under two minutes.
🎥
Watch me build a real breakfast
Full walkthrough. Greek yogurt, protein powder, fiber, banana. Weighed and logged start to finish.

What About Casseroles and Mixed Dishes?

They are harder. That is not a reason to avoid tracking, it is a reason to start somewhere else.

Start with meals you assemble. Bowls, plates, anything where the ingredients go in separately. Once the habit is automatic, mixed dishes and recipes are a small step, and we work through those in the community.

If you learn one skill this year, make it this one.

Lesson Seven · 5 min

Consistency Over
accuracy

Most tracking advice demands perfection. Perfection is exactly why people quit in week two.

I teach consistency over accuracy.

That is not the popular position. Most macro content acts like a missed gram is a failed day.

Here is the truth. Somebody who logs imperfectly every single day for a month beats somebody who logs perfectly for four days and quits. It is not close. The data does not have to be flawless to be useful, it just has to exist.

Chasing perfect
Every bite measured. Every gram exact. Restaurant meals cause panic. Quits by week three.
Chasing consistent
Logs every day, estimates when it has to. Still logging a year later, and knows their food cold.

Awareness, Not Punishment

This is a tool for awareness. It is not a scoreboard for your character.

There is no good food and bad food here. There is no good person and bad person. There is data, and data tells you what to do next. The moment tracking becomes a way to feel guilty about yourself, it stops working and you stop doing it.

You are gathering information. That is all. Stay neutral about it.

Start Small

You do not have to track everything on day one. That is how people overwhelm themselves out of the habit before it forms.

Track one meal a day. Or track only protein. Or log breakfast for a week and add lunch after. Whatever you will actually do, do that. Build the habit first, add precision later.

Log On the Spot

Do not save it for the end of the day. You will not remember, you will guess, and the guess will be wrong in the same direction it is always wrong.

Log it while it is in front of you. Ten seconds now beats five minutes of reconstruction tonight.

Reference

Common questions

Do I have to weigh my food forever?
No. You weigh long enough to calibrate your eye and build the skill, usually a few weeks. Once you know what 150g of chicken actually looks like, you have that for life. Most people find it takes two to five minutes a meal and stops feeling like work fast.
My number looks too high. Is it wrong?
Probably not. Most people over 40 are eating less than they think and moving less than they think, which is a different problem than a slow metabolism. Run your number honestly for three weeks and let the scale tell you. Data beats a hunch every time.
What if I am not losing weight?
Check tracking accuracy before you cut anything. Research consistently shows people underestimate their intake by 30 to 50 percent. Weigh every bite honestly for one week. If the tracking is genuinely tight and the scale still has not moved after three to four weeks, then pull carbs down by 25 to 50 grams.
Do I need to hit my macros perfectly every day?
No. Consistency beats accuracy. Hit protein, get close on the rest, keep showing up. Someone who logs imperfectly every day for a month gets far better results than someone who logs perfectly for four days and stops.
Which app should I use?
Cronometer. The food database is more accurate and it tracks fiber and micronutrients properly, which matters more after 40. That said, any app you will actually open every day beats the perfect app you abandon in a week.
What about eating out, alcohol, and holidays?
That is the nuance layer, and it is genuinely where you need a coach rather than a formula. Bring those to the community feed or a live call and we will work through your actual week instead of a hypothetical one. That is what the community is for.
How often should I recalculate?
Every three to four weeks, or after roughly ten pounds of change. Your TDEE moves as your weight moves. A number that was right in January is not automatically right in March. The calculator remembers your baseline and will walk you through the check-in.
Is it normal for this to feel like a lot at first?
Completely. Every new skill feels clumsy before it feels automatic. You were not good at driving the first week either. Start small, log one meal, and let it get easier. It does.
